Albania did not introduce Diella as a joke. It introduced her as a cure.

In September 2025, Prime Minister Edi Rama elevated Diella, an AI avatar in traditional dress, to the role of Minister of State for Artificial Intelligence. The public promise was simple and powerful: use AI to clean up public procurement. No cousins, no envelopes, no favors, no political debt.

That is exactly why Diella deserves harder questions.

Can anyone see what Diella actually does? Where is the contract? What did it cost? Which tenders has it touched? Where are the logs? Who signs when she is wrong?

And the bluntest question: is Diella a real procurement watchdog, or a chatbot in costume sold as the future of governance?

We do not know enough to call Diella cheap, fake, or corrupt. That would require evidence the public has not seen. What can be said is narrower and more important: the government has not published a standalone Diella cost, contract, audit, vendor list, tender-level decision trail, or public log that would let citizens inspect the anti-corruption claim.

The public can see an avatar, a cloud-hosted assistant, and a promise. It cannot yet see an auditable procurement system.

## What Diella Is Meant To Be

In a government description reported by Slow Media Albania, Diella is an AI system developed by AKSHI, the National Agency for Information Society, and integrated into the e-Albania public services platform. TIME reported that the system uses OpenAI technology through Microsoft's Azure platform, with AKSHI building workflows and scripts around it.

That does not make Microsoft or OpenAI suspect. It means the anti-corruption minister depends on infrastructure whose inner workings are not public. Diella is not a sovereign Albanian model running on public infrastructure that citizens can inspect.

More importantly, the government has said Diella does not make administrative or legal decisions autonomously. Formal responsibility remains with the person or institution using it. TIME also reported that human experts are expected to sign off in the process.

That admission changes the story.

If a human still signs the procurement decision, Diella has not removed the official who can be pressured, bribed, favored, threatened, or politically steered. The corruption risk has not vanished. It has moved into a new interface.

Public procurement corruption is not defeated by saying an AI cannot take cash. It is defeated by creating a record: who drafted the criteria, who evaluated the bid, who changed the recommendation, who approved the award, and who can be punished if the process was rigged.

An anti-corruption AI must make that chain easier to see. Diella does not, publicly.

## Why This Is A European Question

This is not only an Albanian story. Albania is negotiating into the European Union. On May 26, 2026, the Council of the EU said talks had reached closing benchmarks for Cluster 1 "Fundamentals", including public procurement.

The EU AI Act does not appear to list tender evaluation as a standalone high-risk category, so Diella should not be labeled high-risk as settled law. But the Act's high-risk rulebook is the obvious comparison point: risk management, documentation, logging, transparency, human oversight, registration, and impact assessment.

Albania still has no dedicated AI statute, according to a June 2026 legal guide. The Council of Europe AI Convention page lists Albania as a signatory and the European Union as the listed party; the convention points toward challenge rights, transparency, oversight and accountability.

If Albania wants European credibility, Diella has to answer European questions.

## The Institution Behind Diella

AKSHI is not a background IT office. It runs e-Albania, manages major state digital infrastructure, supports online public services, and reports directly to the prime minister. It is also the institution behind Diella.

AKSHI appears again in the World Bank story. Project P177845 is not a Diella contract and should not be described as one. It is a broader World Bank-supported GovTech operation for Albania, implemented by AKSHI, that expanded into AI-enabled e-procurement.

Those documents show real money, planning and procurement architecture. They do not show what Diella cost, who built the avatar-minister system, what Albania pays for Azure or OpenAI-related services, who has audit rights, or what exactly happens when the system touches a public tender.

AKSHI's own 2025 annual report does not solve that. It says Diella was upgraded to version 3.0 and records 1,431,119 interactions and 61,042 automatically generated documents. It also reports 7,141,663,646 lek in realized spending for goods and other services, including system services and Microsoft licences. Diella is not broken out as a costed project.

Then comes the institutional problem.

In 2022, Iranian state-linked hackers hit Albanian government systems in a major cyberattack. A CISA/FBI advisory later attributed the operation to Iranian state actors. Albania cut diplomatic relations with Iran over it. AKSHI sat at the center of the country's digital vulnerability.

Then, in December 2025, SPAK announced a criminal proceeding involving eight suspects and alleged manipulation of public tenders. The SPAK notice described alleged offences around tender equality, structured criminal group activity and related conduct. In April 2026, SPAK announced asset seizures worth more than 40 million euro in the same proceeding.

These are allegations, not convictions. The accused are entitled to the presumption of innocence. SPAK's own notice says as much.

But the sequence is impossible to ignore: a hacked digital state, an AI anti-corruption minister, and then a major corruption proceeding around public tenders involving the state technology ecosystem.

If AKSHI is the spine of Albania's digital government, then Diella sits on that spine. That makes independent auditing essential.

## The Technical Investigation

Security researcher Patrick Quirk, writing as Ringmast4r, asked the technical question directly.

On May 13, 2026, he published an OSINT investigation titled "The AI Minister's Blind Spot: How Albania's Anti-Corruption Bot Can't See Its Own Parliament's Open API." He asked what could be learned from Diella's public-facing frontend and related government systems without credentials, intrusion, or bypassing access controls.

His finding was stark. The public Diella frontend, he reported, was a small Vue.js and Quasar application hosted on Azure Static Web Apps. In its JavaScript bundle, he reported finding no visible AI logic, no model endpoint, no OpenAI reference, no Azure AI service reference, and no public backend URL. AKSHI's broader infrastructure appeared locked behind internal-only systems such as authentication services, GraphQL components, and private APIs.

That does not prove no private backend exists. It does show that the public-facing system does not let citizens, bidders, journalists, or auditors see how Diella reasons, what she calls, what she logs, or whether a human follows her.

Quirk's investigation also moved beyond Diella. He reported finding unauthenticated endpoints and Azure Blob Storage links in the Albanian Parliament's public web infrastructure, exposing a large public document catalog, including parliamentary documents, salary spreadsheets, and other records. His methodology said the work used public endpoints, standard HTTP requests, JavaScript bundle analysis and exposed file URLs, without credentials or access-control bypass.

This is not a government audit. It is an outside OSINT investigation. But if a researcher can examine the public surface and find that the celebrated AI minister is not publicly auditable, the state cannot simply ask citizens to trust the branding.

Maybe Diella's meaningful logic is hidden elsewhere. Then where is the audit?

Maybe the frontend is only a political face over a real procurement engine. Then where are the logs?

Maybe the World Bank-backed platform is the serious system and Diella is the public avatar. Then why sell the avatar as the minister?

## The Missing Trail

An anti-corruption tool does not become credible because a prime minister gives it a name. It becomes credible when losing bidders, auditors, prosecutors, journalists and citizens can inspect the trail.

For Diella, that trail is still missing:

- no published standalone contract;
- no clear public cost;
- no tender-level logs;
- no visible appeal path;
- no independent audit;
- no public vendor and maintenance breakdown;
- no autonomous legal authority;
- no clear map of which humans are accountable at each decision point.

Human officials still sign. AKSHI remains central. And the system's most visible feature, its face, belongs to actress Anila Bisha, who says she agreed to an e-Albania assistant, not a cabinet minister.

The case for Diella was that software could remove human weakness from procurement. But corruption is not only a moral weakness inside a person. It is a weakness in a system: hidden criteria, hidden contracts, hidden influence and hidden accountability.

Replace those hidden parts with an unaudited AI avatar and you have not solved the problem. You have modernized the costume.

Until Albania publishes the contracts, logs, audits, vendors, decision rules, appeal mechanisms and accountable humans behind Diella, the question remains simple.

Is this the future of clean government, or opacity with a better interface?
