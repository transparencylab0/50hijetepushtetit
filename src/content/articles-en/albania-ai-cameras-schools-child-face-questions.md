Before the $140 million. Before the classified contract. Before the Abu Dhabi loan. Before the 5,000 cameras across 20 cities.

Start with the children.

On July 16, 2026, the Albanian government announced that Smart City has begun. Phase 1 covers 20 schools in Tirana. The official explanation is simple: safety, prevention, faster police response, fewer tragedies.

Nobody is against school safety. That is not the question.

The question is what kind of machine Albania is placing inside schools before the public has seen the contract, the data rules, the retention schedule, the access controls or the Data Protection Commissioner's full legal assessment.

Because these are not old hallway cameras that sit silently until someone rewinds the footage after a fight. The government says the new system uses artificial intelligence to analyse potentially dangerous situations in real time and alert monitoring centres. It says the technology can identify dangerous objects, track vehicles linked to criminal activity, locate wanted persons and monitor violations.

That is not passive security. That is live state monitoring.

And now the pilot population is children.

The uncomfortable questions are not only about cameras. They are about the whole surveillance stack around them:

- the facial-recognition technology in the vendor ecosystem;
- the national biometric identity database sitting under the same state architecture;
- the phone-extraction and digital-forensics tools Presight markets to law enforcement;
- the media-monitoring tools Presight markets for sentiment and reputation tracking;
- the command centre where all these signals can become one picture of a person.

The government has not publicly said all these tools are included in Albania's contract. That is the point. The public has not seen enough to know what is included, what is excluded, what is technically disabled and what can be switched on later.

## The headline is schools. The real story is identity.

The safest version of the government's claim is this: AI cameras will detect knives, unauthorized persons, dangerous behaviour and other risks in shared school spaces.

Even that version raises serious questions. Who defines dangerous behaviour? How accurate is the system when applied to children? What happens after a false alert? Who can view the footage? How long is it kept? Can police search it later? Can parents request access? Can a teacher, parent, officer or political actor misuse it?

But the bigger concern is not only what has been publicly confirmed. It is what has not been publicly ruled out.

Smart City is part of a broader architecture. The same project is planned for streets, roads, public spaces, traffic management, body cameras and police command centres. Presight describes the Albania project as a nationwide smart-city transformation across 20 cities, with a national AI platform for digital urban operations, intelligent traffic management and city-scale emergency intelligence.

This is why Albanians should not ask only, "Are there cameras in schools?"

They should ask:

- Is facial recognition enabled in any part of the school pilot?
- If not, is it technically disabled, contractually banned or merely not mentioned?
- Can school footage be searched later against a watchlist?
- Can children's faces be converted into biometric templates?
- Can those templates be retained, transferred or matched later against other databases?
- Which state authority can access the footage?
- Which vendor staff can access the system remotely?
- Where is the data processed?
- What happens when a child is wrongly flagged?

If the answer is "trust us," that is not a safeguard. It is the absence of one.

## "AI cameras" is a softer phrase for a harder reality.

The government has not publicly confirmed that facial recognition is active inside the 20 schools. That should be stated clearly.

But the public record also shows why the question cannot be dismissed as paranoia.

Presight has a commercial partnership with ISS, the company behind the SecurOS video and sensor platform. Presight has said it will deploy ISS's SecurOS platform across UAE national and international projects. ISS markets SecurOS FaceX as a facial-recognition system that supports unlimited database sizes.

That does not prove FaceX is switched on in Albanian schools. It proves the broader vendor ecosystem contains the exact kind of biometric capability Albanians should be asking about before children become the first visible pilot population.

The government should publish a simple answer:

No facial recognition in schools. No biometric templates of children. No watchlist matching. No later biometric search. No transfer to foreign servers. No vendor access without logged, audited, legally authorized purpose.

If those sentences are true, publish them.

If they are not true, parents should know that too.

## Albania is building surveillance before it has written the rules.

The EU AI Act treats biometric identification as one of the most sensitive uses of artificial intelligence. Real-time remote biometric identification in publicly accessible spaces for law enforcement is prohibited, except under narrow conditions. Other biometric systems can fall into high-risk categories. Emotion recognition in education institutions is banned under the EU framework.

Albania is an EU candidate country. It says it is aligning with European standards. Yet Smart City is moving faster than the legal framework around biometric surveillance, child data, AI governance and public-sector algorithmic accountability.

That matters because children cannot meaningfully opt out.

Adults can avoid a square, skip a stadium or choose a different route home. A child must go to school. If a school becomes a data-collection environment, the child is not consenting. The child is complying with compulsory attendance.

That changes the moral and legal weight of the project.

If Albania wants AI in schools, the minimum conditions should be public:

- a child rights impact assessment;
- a data protection impact assessment;
- a list of enabled and disabled AI functions;
- a strict ban on biometric identification of children unless a specific law authorizes it under narrow conditions;
- a retention schedule for video and alerts;
- role-based access rules;
- audit logs;
- independent security testing;
- parental information that explains the system without propaganda;
- a complaint and correction procedure for false alerts;
- clear sanctions for misuse.

None of this is radical. It is the price of putting state AI systems around minors.

## "Parents will be informed" is not the same as accountability.

Prime Minister Edi Rama's July 16 statement says parents should remain informed through parent councils, not specifically about their own child, but about how the system is functioning and how it can be improved.

That may sound reassuring. It is not enough.

Information through parent councils is not the same as legal control over data. It is not the same as knowing whether a child's face is being analysed. It is not the same as knowing whether an alert becomes a police record. It is not the same as knowing whether a vendor can access footage, whether a school administrator can replay it, or whether police can search it months later.

Parents do not need a speech. They need a protocol.

They need to know whether footage is live-viewed, recorded, searchable, exportable, shared with police, shared with the vendor, linked to body cameras, linked to traffic cameras, linked to wanted-person databases or linked to the national identity infrastructure.

The government has presented Smart City as a safety system. Fine. Then publish the safety limits.

## The national ID question cannot be ignored.

Albania is also moving through a broader digital identity transformation. IdentiTek, under the Albanian state, handles biometric identity documents. DPI Map identifies Albania's national digital identity system as connected to the Ministry of Interior, with IdentiTek and IDEMIA identified in the technical support ecosystem.

That does not prove Smart City has access to the national biometric ID database.

But it creates the central question:

Can Smart City cameras, now or later, be matched against state-held identity photos?

If the answer is no, the government should say so in writing and publish the legal and technical firewall.

If the answer is yes, Albania is not merely deploying cameras. It is deploying an identification layer over public life.

And if that layer begins with schools, the public deserves to know before the system normalizes itself around children.

## The phone-spying question: is Marble in the deal?

Presight also markets Marble, a digital forensics platform for law enforcement and investigations. Marble can ingest outputs from tools such as UFED, EnCase and FTK. Presight says it can analyse faces, voices, devices, documents, financial trails and geolocation evidence in a unified workspace.

There is no public proof that Marble is included in Albania's Smart City contract.

That is exactly why the contract matters.

If Marble is not included, publish the exclusion.

If it is included, this is no longer only a camera story. It becomes a phone story too.

The question is whether police can combine camera data, seized-device data, geolocation data, photos, videos, contact networks and message-derived intelligence into one investigative platform.

This is not a theoretical privacy debate. Imagine a protest. Imagine police seize a phone. Imagine extracted photos, contacts and locations are analysed next to street-camera footage and body-camera footage. Imagine the system maps who stood near whom, who called whom and who appeared in whose images.

If Albania is not buying that capability, say so.

If Albania is buying it, the public has a right to debate it before it becomes normal.

The public questions are precise:

- Is Presight Marble included in the Smart City contract or any related agreement?
- Are UFED, EnCase, FTK or other device-extraction outputs meant to feed into the same platform?
- Can faces found inside a seized phone be searched against camera footage?
- Can contacts, locations, photos and videos from a phone be linked to movement data from public cameras?
- What warrant, court order or legal threshold is required before phone data enters the system?
- Who audits those searches after they happen?

## The media problem is not separate either.

Presight also markets NewsPulse, an AI-powered media-monitoring product that tracks large volumes of news in many languages and regions, with sentiment analysis, reputation monitoring and public-perception reporting.

Again, there is no public proof that Albania's Interior Ministry bought NewsPulse as part of Smart City.

But again, the correct question is simple:

Is any media-monitoring, sentiment-tracking or reputation-monitoring product included in the Smart City package, adjacent agreements, training, support or subscriptions?

Because the same state that installs AI cameras in schools and public spaces should not also be quietly buying tools to monitor how journalists, parents and critics talk about those cameras.

If NewsPulse is not included, publish the exclusion.

If it is included, publish who uses it, what it monitors, whether political reporting is included and whether public money is being used to track public criticism.

The same logic applies here: a government should not install a national AI surveillance network and then secretly buy tools to measure public anger about that network.

## Where is the data?

The government and vendor language around "sovereign" AI sounds clean. But sovereignty is not a slogan. It is a server, a jurisdiction, a key-management policy, an access log and a court order.

Albania should publish:

- whether all Smart City data is processed in Albania;
- whether any processing happens in the UAE, the EU or another jurisdiction;
- whether vendor staff can remotely access Albanian camera feeds or analytics;
- whether source code, models and logs are auditable by Albanian authorities;
- whether data can be used to train vendor systems;
- whether the system can operate if foreign support is cut off;
- whether encryption keys are controlled by Albania alone.

If the answer is "classified," then the public is being asked to accept mass monitoring without the basic facts needed to judge it.

That is not sovereignty. That is a blindfold.

## Albania has already learned what happens when data leaks.

Albania's public digital systems have been repeatedly targeted in recent years. Stolen Albanian data has appeared in public and criminal channels. The lesson should be obvious: do not build a more sensitive national database until the public can see how it will be protected.

Names and ID numbers are already dangerous when leaked.

Movement histories, school footage, faces, vehicle routes, police body-camera footage and AI alerts are worse.

If Smart City is breached, the leak is not just a spreadsheet. It can become a map of where people were, when they were there, who they were near and what the system thought they were doing.

For children, that risk is intolerable without strict safeguards.

## The questions parents should ask now.

Parents should not have to become cybersecurity lawyers to understand what is happening in their children's schools. The government should answer plainly.

Here are the questions every school, parent council, journalist and MP should ask:

1. Are the cameras in the 20 Tirana schools capable of facial recognition?
2. Is facial recognition enabled today?
3. If it is disabled, who can enable it and under what legal procedure?
4. Are children's faces converted into biometric templates?
5. Are any images or templates matched against police, civil registry, identity-card or wanted-person databases?
6. How long is footage retained?
7. How long are AI alerts retained?
8. Who can view live footage?
9. Who can export footage?
10. Can police search school footage after the fact?
11. Can vendor staff access footage or system logs?
12. Where is the data processed and stored?
13. Has a data protection impact assessment been completed?
14. Has a child rights impact assessment been completed?
15. Has the Data Protection Commissioner issued a written opinion?
16. What happens when a child is wrongly flagged?
17. Can parents file a complaint or request deletion?
18. Are emotion recognition, behavioural scoring or risk scoring disabled in schools?
19. Are any Smart City data streams linked with body cameras or mobile police systems?
20. What independent audit will be published?

These are not anti-technology questions.

They are the minimum questions in a democratic country.

## The cameras are already in. The answers are not.

The government wants the public to see school AI cameras as protection. Many parents, after real violence and real fear, may want to believe that.

But safety without limits becomes control.

And when the first phase of a national surveillance project begins with children, the burden of proof is on the state.

Not on parents.

Not on journalists.

Not on civil society.

The state must prove that the system is narrow, lawful, necessary, proportionate, secure and independently audited.

Until then, the question is not whether Albania has put cameras in schools.

It has.

The question is what those cameras can become once everyone stops asking.
